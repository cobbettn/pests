Pennsylvania Invasive Insect Pests

Hemlock Woolly Adelgid Spotted Lanternfly

Hemlock Woolly Adelgid (*Adelges tsugae*)

Lifecycle and Identifying Features

The Hemlock Woolly Adelgid (HWA) has two generations per year. The overwintering generation begins feeding in fall and lays eggs in spring. The eggs hatch into crawlers, which spread to new trees. Nymphs settle at the base of needles and begin feeding, eventually forming their distinctive white, woolly masses.

Host and Damage

Hosts: Primarily Eastern and Carolina Hemlocks.
Damage: HWA feeds on tree sap at the base of needles, disrupting nutrient flow and causing needle loss, branch dieback, and eventual tree mortality.

Infestation Symptoms

White, cotton-like woolly masses at the base of needles, premature needle drop, thinning canopy, and eventual tree death within 4-10 years.

Management

  • Monitoring: Regularly inspect undersides of hemlock branches for white woolly masses.
  • Cultural Controls: Maintain tree health through proper watering, mulching, and pruning.
  • Chemical Controls: Systemic insecticides like imidacloprid and dinotefuran provide effective long-term control.
  • Biological Controls: Predatory beetles (*Laricobius nigrinus* and *Sasajiscymnus tsugae*) have been released to help control HWA populations.

Spotted Lanternfly (*Lycorma delicatula*)

Lifecycle and Identifying Features

Eggs hatch in late spring, producing small black nymphs with white spots. As they mature, they develop red patches before becoming adults in mid-summer. Adults have distinctive spotted wings and bright red hindwings. They lay eggs in fall on smooth surfaces, including trees, rocks, and outdoor furniture.

Host and Damage

Hosts: Prefers Tree of Heaven but also attacks grapevines, fruit trees, and hardwoods.
Damage: Feeds on plant sap, weakening plants, reducing fruit yield, and excreting honeydew, which promotes sooty mold growth.

Infestation Symptoms

Clusters of egg masses on tree trunks, sticky honeydew secretions attracting ants and bees, black sooty mold covering leaves and bark, and wilting or leaf curling in heavily infested trees.

Management

  • Monitoring: Check trees and surfaces for egg masses and nymphs.
  • Cultural Controls: Remove Tree of Heaven to limit breeding sites.
  • Chemical Controls: Contact insecticides such as bifenthrin and systemic insecticides like dinotefuran can be effective.
  • Biological Controls: Researchers are studying natural predators such as parasitic wasps (*Anastatus orientalis*) for potential control.
